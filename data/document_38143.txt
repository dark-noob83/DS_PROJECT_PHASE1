Cellular respiration is the set of metabolic reactions and processes that take place in the cells of organisms to convert biochemical energy from nutrients into adenosine triphosphate (ATP), and then release waste products. Fermentation is less efficient at using the energy from glucose: only 2 ATP are produced per glucose, compared to the 38 ATP per glucose nominally produced by aerobic respiration. This is because the waste products of fermentation still contain chemical potential energy that can be released by oxidation.
The most important waste produced during cellular respiration in your cells is carbon dioxide. Each molecule of glucose, one of the most common simple sugars, yields six molecules of carbon dioxide during aerobic respiration--i.e., cellular respiration involving oxygen. Water is another waste byproduct of cellular respiration. Breakdown of one glucose molecule through aerobic respiration yields six molecules of water. Unlike carbon dioxide, however, water isn't a waste that requires any special handling since your cells and bloodstream are already mostly water.

Carbon Dioxide. Carbon dioxide is a waste product of aerobic respiration. The first carbon dioxide production occurs when the products created from glucose during glycolysis are converted into the initial molecule needed to begin the Kreb's cycle. The three key steps of aerobic respiration are glycolysis where the sugar glucose is broken down, the Kreb's cycle where products from glycolysis are converted to other molecules and cellular energy and the electron-transport chain where molecules from the Kreb's cycle split apart to fuel the cell.
