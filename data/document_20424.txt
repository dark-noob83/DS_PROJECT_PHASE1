Sunflower seeds nutrition facts. Sweet, nutty sunflower seeds are an excellent source of essential fatty acids, vitamins, and minerals. Sunflower kernels actually employed to extract edible oil at commercial levels. Besides being eaten as popular snacks, they are also used in the kitchen to prepare variety of recipes.
The sunflower seed is the fruit of the sunflower (Helianthus annuus). The term sunflower seed is actually a misnomer when applied to the seed in its pericarp (hull). Botanically speaking, it is a cypsela. When dehulled, the edible remainder is called the sunflower kernel or heart. There are three types of commonly used sunflower seeds: linoleic (most common), high oleic, and NuSun.
Leaves of the sunflower can be used as cattle feed, while the stems contain a fiber which may be used in paper production. Long before the coming of the white man and the exploitation of Sunflower-oil, the American Indians were using the seeds of the larger species of Sunflowers as important sources of food.
Taller Edible Cultivars. Taller sunflower varieties are best for producing large quantities of good-sized seeds for snacking. Mammoth Russian grows 12 to 15 feet tall with 15-inch-wide flowers and thin-shelled plump meaty seeds. Giganteus flowers are a foot or more wide on plants at least 12 feet tall.
Sunflower Cultivars. Cultivars best for edible seeds, called confection types, come from two types of cultivars with striped hulls. First are the tall cultivars that are everyone's stereotypical image of sunflowers -- plants considerably taller than a person with dinner-plate sized flower heads.
